Existence

How to say "to be" in Japanese


The existence copulas/irregulars/constructs (aru, iru, and desu, da) are an interesting group of words in Japanese that all generally mean "to be". They aren't easily classified as a group of verbs, espeically when it comes to desu and da.

です/だ - is

Desu/da are used to make an assertion of existence. For example:

はかせです。

Hakase desu.

It's the professor.

Da is a more informal term while desu is more formal. While da is often taught as the informal form of desu in textbooks, the two aren't always interchangeable.

ある - exists (inanimate)

Aru is used for inanimate (not moving) objects. For example:

箱がある。

Hako ga aru.

The box exists/There is a box.

However, it is distinguishable because of its unusual negative form: instead of aranai, it simply conjugates immediately to nai. Depending on the situation, aru can be used to refer to things abstractly or objectively, even if they are animate.

いる - exists (animate)

Iru is used for animate (moving) objects. For example:

猫がいる。

Neko ga iru.

The cat exists/There is a cat.

This sentence means literally "The cat exists", or "There is a cat". Iru is often skewed toward living beings, such as people, regardless of their current state of movement.
